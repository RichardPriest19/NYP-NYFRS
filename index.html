What ITIL Is

ITIL 4 Service Value Chain

Incident Management

Restore normal service as fast as possible. Categorise by impact and urgency. P1 incidents (system down, operational impact) get immediate escalation and dedicated resource.

Problem Management

Find root causes — not just fixes. Prevent recurrence. Distinguish between workarounds (short-term) and permanent resolutions. Reduce incident volume over time.

Change Management

Control risk when changing live systems. Standard, normal and emergency change types. CAB (Change Advisory Board) for high-risk changes. Emergency procedure for critical fixes.

Service Level Agreements

Define what "good" looks like contractually. Uptime targets, response times, resolution targets. For police/fire: SLAs must reflect operational criticality, not office hours.

Continual Improvement

Regular review of service performance. Identify improvement opportunities using data. Seven-step improvement model: identify → define → gather → process → analyse → present → implement.

Service Desk

Single point of contact for all IT requests and incidents. First-line resolution targets, escalation paths, knowledge base. Measure user satisfaction, not just ticket volume.

"What's the difference between an incident and a problem?"

A classic ITIL probe — tests whether you know the framework or just name-drop it.

An incident is any unplanned interruption to a service — the system is down, restore it as fast as possible. A problem is the underlying cause of one or more incidents — investigate it properly to stop it happening again. The danger in reactive IT teams is spending all your time on incidents without ever fixing the underlying problems. You end up patching the same faults repeatedly. My approach is to run robust incident management whilst simultaneously tracking recurring patterns and escalating them into formal problem investigations. Over time, that reduces incident volume. In a police/fire context, that matters enormously — you don't want crews experiencing the same system failures repeatedly because you never addressed root cause.

Core Agile Concepts

Scrum Sprint Cycle

Sprint

A fixed time-box (typically 2 weeks) in which the team delivers a working increment. Non-negotiable duration — if work isn't done, it returns to the backlog. Creates rhythm and predictability.

Product Backlog

The prioritised list of everything the product needs. Owned by the Product Owner. Constantly refined — items at the top are detailed and ready; items lower down are rough. Highest value items go first.

Daily Standup

15-minute daily sync: what did I do yesterday, what am I doing today, what's blocking me? Not a status report — a blocker removal mechanism. Issues flagged immediately, resolved same day.

Retrospective

End of every sprint: what went well, what didn't, what do we change? The engine of continual improvement. Psychological safety essential — blame-free diagnosis of process failure.

Definition of Done

Explicit agreed criteria that must be met before any item is called complete. Prevents "nearly done" creep. In police/fire context: tested, security-reviewed, documented, signed off by operational user.

Agile vs Police/Fire

Pure Agile can conflict with procurement rules, statutory compliance and change control. Solution: Agile inside governance gates. Sprints deliver inside approved change windows. Speed within guardrails.
